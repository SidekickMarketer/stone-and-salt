# Stone & Salt Financial Model
## Commercial-Primary Year 1 Projections | February 15, 2026

---

## Executive Summary

This financial model projects Stone & Salt's path to $325,000 in Year 1 revenue under a commercial-primary positioning strategy. The model accounts for the critical 3-6 month commercial sales cycle, incorporates the signed Mike's Carwash contract as anchor revenue, and uses validated pricing from PRICING_BENCHMARKS.md throughout.

**Key findings:**
- Break-even month: Month 7 (September 2026)
- Cash flow positive: Month 8 (October 2026)
- Working capital required: $38,000-$52,000
- Total startup capital needed: $55,000-$78,000 (conservative-to-buffer)
- Year 1 net profit (conservative): $42,500-$58,000

**Critical assumption:** The 3-6 month commercial sales cycle means months 1-4 will operate at a loss. Hardscape projects and residential maintenance bridge the gap. Mike's Carwash provides immediate recurring revenue.

### 2D. Cash Flow Assumptions

- Commercial grounds contracts: Billed monthly, net-30 payment terms. Assume 15-day average collection.
- Hardscape projects: 50% deposit at signing, 50% on completion. Projects average 3-4 weeks.

**Expense timing:**
- Insurance: Paid quarterly ($3,000/quarter) -- shown as monthly amortization.
- Equipment: $16,500 tractor financed. $450/month for 36 months at 0% (Kubota promo).

**Collections risk:** Commercial clients typically pay net-30. Model assumes 15-day average. A 30-day delay would shift the cash flow negative zone by one month. Hardscape deposits mitigate this risk because 50% is collected before work begins.

## 5. Risk Assessment & Sensitivity Analysis

### 5A. High Risk: 3-6 Month Commercial Sales Cycle

**Impact:** The single biggest threat to Year 1 cash flow. Commercial property managers operate on annual budget cycles. Decision-making involves RFP processes, board approvals, and multi-vendor evaluation. Even a warm lead can take 90-180 days from first conversation to signed contract.

**Cash flow impact:** If the average commercial contract takes 5 months to close (from first contact to first revenue), outreach starting March 1 means first new commercial revenue arrives August at earliest. Mike's Carwash bridges months 1-7, but total commercial revenue is backloaded to Q3-Q4.

**Mitigation strategies:**
1. **Mike's Carwash as anchor:** Already signed. Provides $18,000-$28,000/year (grounds + snow) and -- more importantly -- serves as a reference account for every subsequent proposal.
2. **Hardscape as bridge revenue:** 4-6 residential hardscape projects generate $91,000 with faster sales cycles (2-4 weeks from lead to deposit). This fills the Q1-Q2 cash gap.
3. **Start outreach immediately:** Target 50 property manager conversations by March 15 (per CONCEPT-BRIEF.md success conditions). Volume of conversations compresses the sales cycle.
4. **Budget season targeting:** Q4 is when commercial PMs set next year's budgets. Proposals sent in Oct-Nov position for January starts -- meaning Year 2 commercial pipeline starts building in Year 1 Q3.
5. **Proposal speed advantage:** 48-72 hour proposal turnaround (vs. competitors' 1-2 weeks) compresses the decision timeline.

---

### 5B. Medium Risk: Salt Price Volatility

**Impact:** Cincinnati river terminal closures removed 200,000+ tons of bulk salt capacity. Cargill exited the local market. Salt prices have swung from $67/ton (DOT rate) to $140+/ton on spot markets. A severe winter with tight supply could push prices even higher.

**Financial impact on a $12,000 snow contract:**

| Salt Price | Material Cost | Margin | Profit Loss vs. Baseline |
|-----------|---------------|--------|--------------------------|
| $67/ton (DOT) | $1,800 | 48% | +$300 (baseline+) |
| $75/ton (baseline) | $2,100 | 45% | $0 |
| $100/ton | $2,800 | 40% | -$700 |
| $140/ton | $3,600 | 35% | -$1,500 |
| $175/ton (worst case) | $4,400 | 28% | -$2,300 |

**Impact across 5 snow contracts:** At $140/ton, total profit loss = $7,500 (vs. $75/ton baseline). At $175/ton worst case, loss = $11,500.

**Mitigation strategies:**
1. **Salt surcharge clause in every contract:** "Material costs based on $85/ton bulk rate. Actual costs exceeding $85/ton will be passed through at cost plus 10% handling." This is standard industry practice.
2. **Pre-season bulk purchase:** Buy salt in August-September at pre-season rates. Lock in 20-30 tons before demand spikes.
3. **Diversify suppliers:** Build relationships with 2-3 salt suppliers. The DOT rate ($67/ton) is available to qualified buyers.
4. **Liquid brine alternative:** Pre-treating with brine reduces bulk salt usage by 30-40%. Investment in a brine tank ($1,500-$3,000) pays for itself if salt exceeds $120/ton.

---

### 5C. Medium Risk: Labor Shortage

**Impact:** 80% of landscape operators report hiring challenges (per research). As Stone & Salt grows past 2-partner capacity (likely Month 4-6), hiring reliable crew members becomes critical.

**Financial impact:** Unable to hire = unable to serve additional contracts = revenue cap. If crew hiring delays by 2 months, Q3-Q4 revenue could be reduced by $20,000-$30,000 as contracts are turned away or delayed.

**Mitigation strategies:**
1. **Premium wages:** Pay $18-$22/hour (vs. industry $14-$17). This costs $3,000-$5,000/year more per employee but dramatically improves retention and candidate quality.
2. **Year-round employment:** Offer year-round positions (grounds spring-fall, snow winter). This is the #1 differentiator in hiring -- most landscape companies lay off crews in November.
3. **Partner-first model:** Partners perform all work months 1-4. First hire in Month 5. This delays the labor risk until revenue supports the investment.
4. **Referral bonus:** $500 per hired referral that stays 90 days.

| Month | Headcount | Role | Monthly Labor Cost |

### 5D. Low Risk: Cincinnati Weather (Snow)

**Impact:** Cincinnati averages only 15-23 inches of snow per year (below the national average of 28 inches). Some winters bring 10 inches; rare winters bring 30+. Building a business model on snow revenue in Cincinnati is structurally unreliable.

**Financial impact of low-snow winter:**

| Snowfall | Snow Revenue (seasonal contracts) | Snow Revenue (per-push overflow) | Total Snow Revenue |
|----------|----------------------------------|-----------------------------------|--------------------|
| 10" (mild winter) | $65,000 (contracts pay regardless) | $2,000 | $67,000 |
| 18" (average) | $65,000 | $8,000 | $73,000 |
| 25" (above average) | $65,000 | $15,000 | $80,000 |

**Key insight:** Seasonal contracts pay the same regardless of snowfall. This is why the model uses seasonal contracts as the primary snow revenue structure. Per-push is upside only. In a mild winter, Stone & Salt still collects $65,000 in seasonal contract revenue. In a heavy winter, per-push overflow adds $8,000-$15,000.

**Mitigation:** Snow is already positioned as supplementary (20% of revenue). The model does NOT depend on above-average snowfall. Even zero snow revenue leaves $260,000 in grounds, hardscape, and maintenance -- enough to cover all expenses and provide modest partner income.
